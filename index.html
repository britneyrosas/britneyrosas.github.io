Projects

ROCKET PROPULSION LABORATORY

I joined Rocket Propulsion Laboratory (RPL) in October 2022 as part of the New Member Project (NMP), where I was introduced to hands-on rocket design and engineering. Through NMP, I collaborated closely with other members and gained practical skills in CAD development and simulation setup using tools like OpenRocket and SOLIDWORKS. The project culminated in the successful launch of G-class model rockets at Friends of Amateur Rocketry in California, an experience that solidified my foundational knowledge and passion for rocketry.

After completing NMP, I joined the Osiris Structures team. Osiris is RPL’s new and first multi-stage solid propellant rocket, designed to compete in the 2025 Spaceport America Cup, an intercollegiate rocket engineering competition. As part of the Structures team, I’ve conducted quantitative analyses of materials and geometries for Osiris’s external structure through simulations. My work involves optimizing composite laminates and isotropic components using SOLIDWORKS and ANSYS, including Ansys ACP for failure analysis and performance validation. At our current pace, we aim to finalize design and manufacturing by the end of this school year.

In my current role as Structures Lead Engineer, I oversee the structural design of the rocket and ensure that all components meet rigorous safety and performance standards. As the team's Safety Officer, I maintain a thorough understanding of our manufacturing processes, particularly fiber composite layup procedures, and develop safety protocols to ensure compliance during production. I have also lead sub team training sessions to ensure high-quality fabrication and adherence to safety regulations. Being a part of RPL has been incredibly rewarding. Each new challenge pushes me to expand my skillset beyond my current expertise. What makes Osiris particularly meaningful is the shared ambition to push the limits of what's possible in student rocketry. This is the most long-term project I’ve committed to, and I’m eagerly looking forward to seeing our hard work come to fruition at the 2025 Spaceport America Cup.

GENERAL ATOMICS

My time at General Atomics was an invaluable hands-on learning experience, where I supported the structural analysis of mechanical components for UCAVs (Unmanned Combat Aerial Vehicles). Using NX and Ansys, I was responsible for applying loads, setting boundary conditions, and optimizing mesh models to simulate real-world conditions. What made this experience particularly rewarding was the chance to dive deep into Finite Element Analysis (FEA), where I was able to truly understand the theory I had learned in my classes and see its practical application. For the first time, I wasn’t just learning about FEA in the abstract — I was applying it thoughtfully to models, balancing accuracy with efficiency.

I took away an important lesson from this experience: "All models are trash, but some are useful." As I iterated on designs through model combination methods and optimized through material selection and structural relief cutouts, I learned that no simulation is perfect, but they are invaluable tools when approached with the right mindset. This principle has reshaped my approach to solving real engineering problems and has fueled my enthusiasm for my current work at RPL.

In addition to FEA, I used Excel extensively for data analysis, managing complex kinematic datasets, and generating tolerance reports. This work ensured that subcomponent selections were optimized, aligning with design specifications and ensuring the reliability of the systems we were working on. Exposure to GD&T principles was also a key part of my role, as I learned to balance precision with practicality in design.

I also contributed to internal technical reviews, creating over 90 slides of detailed artifact documentation that supported comprehensive assessments of various components. Lastly, I had the opportunity to oversee Linear Actuator Hardware Testing in GA's testing lab, where I managed test procedures, analyzed results, and ensured thorough documentation — a critical process that ensured the effectiveness of the hardware.
